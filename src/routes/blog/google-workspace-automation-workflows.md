If your business runs on Google Workspace, you're sitting on automation potential you're probably not using. Gmail, Sheets, Drive, Calendar, and Forms can do far more than their default settings suggest — especially when connected to your other tools.

Here are five high-impact automations that work for nearly every SMB running Google Workspace.

## 1. Intake Form to CRM Pipeline

**The manual version:** A potential client fills out a contact form. Someone checks the form responses spreadsheet (or worse, a shared inbox), copies the information into your CRM, tags the lead, and assigns it to a salesperson.

**The automated version:**

Google Form submission triggers:

1. New contact created in your CRM (HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive) with all form fields mapped
2. Lead assigned based on criteria (geography, company size, service interest)
3. Acknowledgment email sent to the prospect within 60 seconds
4. Slack notification to the assigned team member
5. Follow-up task created if no response within 48 hours

**Time saved: 5-10 hours/month** depending on lead volume.

The key insight: speed-to-response matters. Studies consistently show that leads contacted within 5 minutes are 10x more likely to convert than those contacted within 30 minutes. Automation makes sub-minute response the default.

## 2. Automated Reporting from Google Sheets

**The manual version:** Every Monday morning, someone pulls data from multiple Sheets (or exports from other tools into Sheets), consolidates numbers, formats a summary, and emails it to the team.

**The automated version:**

Scheduled workflow runs every Monday at 7 AM:

1. Pulls latest data from source Sheets (or connected systems)
2. Calculates KPIs, compares to targets, flags anomalies
3. Generates a formatted summary (Google Doc or Slides, or directly in email)
4. Sends to distribution list with highlights and exceptions called out
5. Archives report in a shared Drive folder organized by date

**Time saved: 4-8 hours/month.**

Pro tip: Don't just replicate the manual report. Automated reports can include things that were too tedious to calculate by hand — trend analysis, week-over-week comparisons, automatic flagging when metrics cross thresholds.

## 3. Document Approval Workflows

**The manual version:** Someone creates a document (proposal, contract, expense report). They email it to their manager. The manager eventually reviews it, sends feedback via email, the author revises, sends it back. Tracking who approved what and when lives in email threads.

**The automated version:**

Document placed in approval folder triggers:

1. Notification to the designated approver with a direct link
2. Approver reviews and clicks Approve/Reject/Revise in a simple interface
3. Approved documents auto-move to the "approved" folder with a timestamp
4. Rejected documents return to the author with comments
5. Full audit trail logged in a Sheets tracker — who, what, when, decision

**Time saved: 3-6 hours/month** per approval chain.

This one matters most for compliance-heavy businesses. Manual approval tracking via email is a compliance risk. Automated tracking with timestamped logs is an audit asset.

## 4. Calendar-Based Task Triggers

**The manual version:** A meeting ends. Someone needs to send follow-up notes, create action items, update a project tracker, and schedule the next meeting. This usually happens... eventually. Or not.

**The automated version:**

Calendar event ending triggers:

1. Template follow-up email sent to all attendees (editable before send, or fully automatic for recurring meetings)
2. Action items extracted from meeting notes (via a shared Google Doc) and pushed to your task management tool
3. Next meeting auto-scheduled based on cadence rules
4. Meeting summary logged in the project's shared folder

**Time saved: 2-4 hours/month.**

This is especially valuable for client-facing teams. Consistent, prompt follow-up after every meeting builds trust and keeps projects moving.

## 5. Cross-Platform Data Sync

**The manual version:** When a deal closes in your CRM, someone manually updates a Google Sheet tracker, creates a project folder in Drive, adds the client to a mailing list, and updates a capacity planning spreadsheet.

**The automated version:**

Deal-closed event in CRM triggers:

1. Row added to the master client tracker in Sheets
2. Client folder created in Drive from a template (with subfolders for contracts, deliverables, communications)
3. Client added to the appropriate email list
4. Capacity planning sheet updated with projected hours
5. Onboarding workflow kicked off (see automation #1 in reverse)

**Time saved: 3-5 hours/month.**

The real value isn't just time. It's eliminating the "I forgot to update the spreadsheet" problem. When systems sync automatically, your data stays consistent across tools.

## Getting Started

You don't need to build all five at once. Pick the one that causes the most pain or costs the most hours, and start there.

The implementation path:

1. **Map the current workflow** — Every step, every system, every decision point
2. **Identify the trigger** — What event starts the process?
3. **Define the logic** — What happens, in what order, with what conditions?
4. **Build and test** — Start with a test environment before going live
5. **Monitor for two weeks** — Watch for edge cases and adjust

Simple automations (form to CRM, scheduled report) can be built in a day. Complex ones (multi-step approval workflows with conditional logic) take a week or two.
